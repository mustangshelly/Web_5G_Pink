Pygmy Hippopotamus - Choeropsis liberiensis

Is it Just a Baby Hippopotamus?

Although many people believe the Pygmy hippopotamus is a miniature version of the hippo, they are incorrect. The two mammals differ in their physical characteristics and behaviors. Because he Pygmy hippo tends to spend less time in the water its head is rounder and narrower, with a long neck and its eye are on the sides of its face, not on top. Its small body has longer legs than their relatives, the elephants. Pygmy hippos only grow to be half as tall and weigh less than 1/4 than its cousin, the hippopotamus. The average full grown adults stand between 2.46 to 3.28 feet and weigh anywhere between 397 to 606 pounds. Due to the animal's nocturnalism, they were not discovered until 1840 and little know about it habits in the wild. The Pygmy hippos rest during the day hidden away in swamps or rivers and later came out at night to eat during the cool hours of the night. Since they are herbivores their diet consists of leaves, roots, ferns, and fruits near rivers or streams. The Pygmy hippopotamus has a live expectancy of 27 years which is much shorter than their relatives, the hippopotamus, which is 50 years.

Why is it Endangered?

These rare animals can be located in the interior forests in parts of West Africa, mainly in Liberia, but also found in Sierra Leone, Guinea, and the Ivory Coast. The World Conservation Union estimates fewer than 3,000 individuals remaining in the wild. The Pygmy hippo is primarily being threatened due to the loss of their habitat. Since forests are being logged and converted to farmland, and war there are very little designated areas to give these animals shelter. The rivers they swim in are being polluted by human causing harm to the hippos. As forestes are shrinking, the population of these endangered hippos decreased leading to less diversity in the mating pool. They are also very vulnerable to poaching, hunting for bushmeat, and natural predators. In Liberia, these hippos are among the species being illegally hunted for food. Unlike the common hippo, their large teeth have no value.

Conservation

Luckily, Pygmy hippos breed very well in zoos, but more protection is required for the survival of this wild populations so that they will not go extinct. In Switzerland, the Basel Zoo has the international breed registry book and coordinates the entire population of captive freely breds around the world in the different zoos.The population of the Pygmy hippos born in captivity nearly doubled between the years 1970 and 1991. According to the World Conservation Union this species has a greater chance of survival in zoo than in the wild. The Pygmy hippos in captivity live approximately 42 go 55 years longer than in the wild. Recent study has shown that only 41% of all Pygmy born in zoos have been male since 1919. Liberia's Forestry Development Authority and Fauna & Flora International had hosted a nation action planning meeting in December 2012 in which and Action Plan for the Conservation of the Pygmy Hippo in Liberia had been created. This plan adopts the same visions and goals as the Regional Pygmy Hippo Conservation Strategy and articulates objectives and actions necessary to attain those in Liberia. The Regional Pygmy Hippo Conservation Strategy states that there are now conservation or research initiatives targeting Pygmy Hippos in all four West African countries.
